Understanding the mind for societal change.


Online Lecture Series 2021

(23.09. – 21.10.)

The cognitive science research of today is dominated by specific disciplines and methods - especially neuroscience and AI and an empirical approach and a computational paradigm respectively. Many other perspectives are disregarded and receive little to no attention. The ambition of this lecture series is to create a platform for ideas outside the common and settled "cognitive science culture” and to induce a progressive discussion on how to research the mind. We want to integrate new, different and usually disregarded directions (such as alternative epistemologies, critical AI, and critical race theory) within the research field of cognitive science. With the presented talks, we want to make a start and thereby provide an incentive to critically reflect upon the fundamental beliefs and approaches of cognitive science research in general.

14:30h 		Mindshaping, Enactivism, and Ideological Oppression
Michelle Maiese
Abstract

One of humans’ distinctive cognitive abilities is that they develop an array of capacities through an enculturation process. In “Cognition as a Social Skill,” Sally Haslanger (2019) points to one of the dangers associated with enculturation: ideological oppression. To conceptualize how such oppression takes root, Haslanager appeals to notions of mindshaping and social coordination, whereby people participate in oppressive social practices unthinkingly or even willingly. Arguably, an appeal to mindshaping provides a new kind of argument, grounded in philosophy of mind, which supports the claims that feminist and anti-racist want to defend. However, some theorists worry that Haslanger’s account does not shed much light on how individuals could exert their agency to resist oppression. I argue that enactivist conceptions of mindshaping and habit can help us to make sense of the power of social influences and how they have the potential to both enable and undermine cognition and agency. This moves us toward increased understanding of the workings of social oppression—distinguishing between (a) constructive and enabling forms of heteronomy, and (b) overdetermining and pernicious modes that lead to atrophied moral cognition and a narrowing of the field of affordances.
07.10.2021
16:00h 		When Emotions Go Wrong: Epistemic and Ethical Distortions
Mathew Congdon
Abstract

An often-noted strength of cognitive theories of emotion is their ability to explain how particular emotional responses can be criticized on rational grounds. If emotions are either identical to or closely associated with judgments about the world (e.g., that danger is imminent in the case of fear; or that one has been wronged in the case of resentment), then emotions can be subject to rational criticism by showing that their implicit cognitive content implies something false. In such cases, we can say that the emotions are criticized as inapt. This paper explores a second way that emotions can go wrong, namely, by contributing to the simultaneously epistemic and ethical distortion of the agent’s normative outlook. Prolonged bouts of anger, jealousy, hatred, fear, and other emotions can go wrong not only by implying particular false judgments about the world, but by contributing more generally to a pernicious picture of the world in which one sees one’s existing biases and prejudices confirmed as incontrovertible. In this second sort of case, we can say that the emotions are criticized not simply as inapt but as distorting. This paper argues that these two sides of emotions going wrong should be understood in relation to one another and defends a view called the articulation model of emotion that attempts to meet this challenge. According to the articulation model, the cognitive work of emotions consists not only in making particular evaluative judgments, but also in the more fundamental activity of stitching together a value-laden picture of the world in which those judgments can be formulable and intelligible in the first place. In developing this view, I contrast it with other cognitive-evaluative views, like Martha Nussbaum’s, and focus primarily on the case of anger and resentment. I conclude by considering some parallels between distorting emotions and the distorting effects of ideology.
14.10.2021
16:00h		 Extended Machine Intelligence: On the role of human (cognitive) labor in AI systems
Rainer Mühlhoff
Abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI), especially machine learning, structurally dependends on human participation. Technologies such as Deep Learning (DL) leverage networked media infrastructures and human-machine interaction designs to harness users to provide training and verification data. The current success of machine learning is based on a fundamental socio-technological transformation of the relationship between humans and machines. This transformation is driven by current trends in Human-Computer interaction design and perpetuated through digital media culture. In the 1960s, the relationship between humans and AI was shaped by the idea of aspired resemblance between humans and machines. Machine intelligence referred to a machine imitating human cognitive skills such as chess playing or language comprehension. As I will argue in the talk, today, AI does not imitate human intelligence, neither does it replace human work. Rather, AI captures human cognitive, social and affective skills in hybrid human-machine apparatuses, which perform, as a whole, the resulting artificial intelligence. Contemporary machine learning systems thus rely on hidden forms of human participation orchestrated by digital media. This human participation is mostly unpaid and often not recognized as labor. In this talk I will elaborate on what might be called a social theory of contemporary machine intelligence. I differentiate five types of “media technologies of capture” in AI apparatuses and analyze them as forms of power relations between humans and machines. Clickwork (e.g. Amazon Mechanical Turk) or Commercial Content Moderation are only two common forms of this kind of labor. Less well known forms include "social labor" on platforms such as Facebook, and "tracking and trapping" techniques of capture e.g. by Google Search. This talk is organized around examples to critically debate the current hype of machine learning and discuss related forms of social control and discrimination of users.
